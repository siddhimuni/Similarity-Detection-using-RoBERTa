Detroit parks are numerous and their total area is about 1200 acres. By far the most attractive one is Belle Isle, an island in the river in the extreme E. City, bought in 1879 and with an area of more than 700 acres. The Grand Circus Park 4 acres and a half, with its trees, flowers and fountains, offers a pleasant resting place in the busiest quarter of the city. Six miles out in Palmer Park, Woodward Avenue is about 140 hectares, given the city in 1894 and named in honor of the donor. Clark Park (28 acres) is in the west of the city, and there are several smaller parks. Cemeteries are the main Elmwood (Protestant) and Mount Elliott (Catholic), that are adjacent in the E. of the city, in Woodmere W. Woodlawn and in the northern part of the city.

Charity and Education .-- Among the charities are general hospitals (Harper, Grace and St. Mary), the emergence of Detroit, the Free Child and hospitals in the United States Navy, Hospital de San Lucas, the church from home, and the orphanage, the House of Providence (hospital maternity and child asylum); Hospital for Women and the foundling home, a convalescent center for children, & c. In 1894 the mayor, Hazen Pingree Senter (1842-1901) instituted the practice of preparing, through the municipal aid and supervision, large tracts of vacant land in and around the city for growing potatoes and other vegetables and then, in conjunction with the board of commissioners poor, giving it in small lots to families of the unemployed, and providing seed for sowing
